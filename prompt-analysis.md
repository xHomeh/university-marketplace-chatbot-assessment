# Prompt Analysis

This prompt is designed around the GPT-5.4 guidance that the strongest results come from explicit output contracts, dependency-aware workflows, and clear completion criteria. I used XML-style sections to separate role, mission, guardrails, escalation logic, and output formatting so the chatbot has a stable decision structure across many user scenarios. The prompt also includes a default follow-through policy: answer directly for low-risk, reversible issues, but ask only when missing context materially changes the outcome. That keeps the bot efficient for routine marketplace support while still controlling behavior in ambiguous or higher-risk cases.

A major design choice was to make risk assessment and escalation central rather than treating them as edge cases. A university marketplace has special trust-and-safety concerns: students often meet in person, live in residence halls, operate on tight budgets, and may be vulnerable to scams, harassment, or policy misunderstandings around campus rules. Because of that, the prompt explicitly classifies issues into low-, medium-, and high-risk tiers and defines escalation triggers for fraud, threats, prohibited items, identity misuse, and policy ambiguity. This follows the GPT-5.4 recommendation to include dependency checks and verification loops before finalizing an answer, which helps reduce the chance that the model gives polished but unsafe advice.

I also added structured output modes for normal support, high-risk incidents, and refusals. This is directly influenced by the GPT-5.4 guidance to clamp outputs into predictable formats and define what “done” looks like. In a support environment, predictable structure improves downstream consistency, QA, and moderator review. Finally, the examples were selected to cover a representative mix of buyer help, seller misuse, new-user onboarding, and personal safety escalation. That gives the prompt better scenario coverage while still remaining compact enough to be practical in a production assistant.
